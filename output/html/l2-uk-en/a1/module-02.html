### Completing Your Alphabet

In Module 01, you learned 18 letters. Now let's complete the Ukrainian alphabet with the remaining 15 letters. After this module, you'll be able to read ANY Ukrainian word!

Today's letters include some special ones that make Ukrainian unique - letters that don't exist in Russian or other Slavic languages.

> üîç **Myth Buster: "Ukrainian is a Russian Dialect"**
>
> Some people claim Ukrainian is just "a dialect of Russian." This is **completely false** - and politically charged misinformation. Ukrainian and Russian are separate languages that split from a common ancestor about 1,000 years ago. They have:
> - Different sounds and phonology
> - Different grammar rules
> - Different vocabulary (only ~60% lexical similarity)
> - **Different letters** - Ukrainian has –á, –Ñ, “ê that Russian lacks!
>
> Would you call Spanish a "dialect of Italian"? Same logic applies here.

> üí° **Did You Know?**
>
> The letter **“ê** (hard G) has a fascinating history! It was **banned** during the Soviet era as part of Russification policies. After Ukrainian independence in 1991, it was officially restored. Today, words with “ê like **“ë–∞–Ω–æ–∫** (porch) and **“ë—É–¥–∑–∏–∫** (button) are markers of authentic Ukrainian spelling. When you see “ê, you're seeing a letter that survived cultural suppression!

> ‚ö° **Pro Tip: –ì vs “ê**
>
> - **–ì** = soft H sound (like "hello") - VERY common
> - **“ê** = hard G sound (like "go") - rare, mostly in borrowed words
>
> Russian has only –ì (pronounced as G), so Russians often mispronounce Ukrainian –ì. This is a telltale accent marker!

Group 5: Iotated Vowels (4 letters)

These vowels start with a "Y" sound (like "yes"):

| Letter | Sound | Example |
|--------|-------|---------|
| –Ñ —î | YE | "YE" in "yes" |
| –á —ó | YI | "YEE" (yi as in "yeast") |
| –Æ —é | YU | "YU" in "universe" |
| –Ø —è | YA | "YA" in "yard" |

> üìú **History Bite: The Iotated Vowels**
>
> The term "iotated" comes from the Greek letter **iota** (Œπ). These vowels combine a "y" glide (called "iot") with a regular vowel:
> - –Ñ = –ô + –ï (y + e)
> - –á = –ô + –Ü (y + i)
> - –Æ = –ô + –£ (y + u)
> - –Ø = –ô + –ê (y + a)
>
> After a consonant, they soften that consonant instead of adding "y". This is key to Ukrainian pronunciation!

> üéØ **Fun Fact: –á - Ukraine's Signature Letter**
>
> The letter **–á** exists ONLY in Ukrainian - no other language in the world uses it! It's so iconic that there's a famous Ukrainian cultural magazine called "–á" (Yi). When you see this letter, you know 100% you're reading Ukrainian, not Russian, Bulgarian, or any other Cyrillic language.

Group 6: Special Letters (4 letters)

| Letter | Sound | Function |
|--------|-------|----------|
| –ò –∏ | Y (short) | Like "i" in "bit" (not "ee"!) |
| –ô –π | Y (consonant) | Like "y" in "yes" |
| –¨ —å | ‚Äî (silent) | Softens the previous consonant |
| ' | ‚Äî (pause) | Apostrophe - separates sounds |

> üîó **Language Link: –ò vs –Ü vs –á**
>
> Ukrainian has THREE "i" sounds - confusing at first, but logical:
> - **–Ü** = "ee" as in "m**ee**t" (True Friend from Module 01)
> - **–ò** = short "i" as in "b**i**t" (no English equivalent - between "i" and "u")
> - **–á** = "yee" as in "**yea**st" (y + ee combined)
>
> Listen carefully to native speakers - these are distinct sounds!

> üí° **Did You Know? The Silent Letter**
>
> The **soft sign (–¨)** makes no sound by itself! Instead, it **changes** the consonant before it:
> - **–∫–æ–Ω** (horse) ‚Üí **–∫—ñ–Ω—å** (different horse form, softer ending)
> - **–º–∞—Ç** (checkmate) ‚Üí **–º–∞—Ç—å** (mother - archaic)
>
> Think of –¨ as an instruction: "soften what came before me!"

### The Cursive Trap

**WARNING:** Ukrainian cursive (italic) letters can look VERY different from print!

| Print | Cursive | Trap Level |
|-------|---------|------------|
| —Ç | m-like | ‚ö†Ô∏è HIGH - looks like Latin "m"! |
| –ø | n-like | ‚ö†Ô∏è HIGH - looks like Latin "n"! |
| –¥ | g-like | ‚ö†Ô∏è HIGH - looks like Latin "g"! |
| –∏ | u-like | ‚ö†Ô∏è MEDIUM - looks like Latin "u" |
| –ª | hook | ‚ö†Ô∏è MEDIUM - connected differently |
| –≥ | ‚àÇ-like | ‚ö†Ô∏è MEDIUM - loops differently |

> üé≠ **Culture Corner: Handwriting in Ukraine**
>
> Ukrainians learn cursive writing in first grade and use it throughout life. Official documents, signatures, and handwritten notes are almost always in cursive. If you ever receive a handwritten letter from Ukraine, remember: that "m" is probably **—Ç**, that "n" is probably **–ø**, and that "g" is probably **–¥**!
