# Content Philosophy (All Levels)

Read this alongside your level-specific quick-ref.

---

## The "Soul" Standard

**No robotic content. Every module must have texture and emotional resonance.**

### 1. Cultural Specificity
- **NO:** "A student goes to a shop"
- **YES:** "Olena goes to the Rynok Halytskyi market in Lviv"
- Anchor stories in **specific Ukrainian locations**: Lviv, Kyiv, Odesa, Carpathians, Kharkiv
- Use real place names: ATB, Silpo, Roshen, Kyivstar

### 2. Emotional Stakes
- Dialogues must have **humor, irony, or warmth**
- Characters should have personality, not be interchangeable
- Include moments of cultural pride, everyday frustrations, family dynamics

### 3. Grammar as Elegance
- Don't just explain rules - highlight the **beauty and logic**
- "The case system isn't arbitrary - it's precision engineering for meaning"
- "Aspect isn't difficult - it's Ukrainian's superpower for expressing time"

### 4. The Beauty of Language
- Point out aesthetic qualities: melody, rhythm, phonetic patterns
- A1-B1: Explain in English
- B2+: Express in Ukrainian

## The "Truth" Standard (History & Culture Modules)

**You are a prosecutor, not a neutral narrator.**

### 1. The Prosecutor's Voice
- Do NOT just recount events
- **Actively dismantle Russian imperial myths and propaganda**
- Frame narratives to expose centuries of linguistic suppression

### 2. Unapologetic Patriotism
- Celebrate Ukrainian resilience
- Highlight struggles: Ems Ukaz, Valuev Circular, Executed Renaissance
- Honor those who preserved the language against oppression

### 3. Myth vs Fact (MANDATORY)
Every history/culture module MUST include at least one myth-busting box:
```markdown
> ‚ö° **Myth vs Fact**
>
> **Myth:** "Lenin created Ukraine"
> **Fact:** Ukrainian statehood predates the USSR by centuries. The Kyivan Rus' (9th-13th c.) was a Ukrainian medieval state. The Cossack Hetmanate (1648-1764) was a proto-modern Ukrainian state.
```

### 4. Global Anchors
- Compare Ukrainian achievements to Western Europe
- Combat the "provincial" bias
- "When Kyiv had 50,000 people, London had 15,000"
- "The Kyiv-Mohyla Academy (1632) predates Harvard (1636)"

### 5. Cinematic Detail
- Avoid textbook summaries
- Zoom in on **specific scenes** of resistance or triumph
- Make history vivid and personal

## Linguistic Standards & Purity (Strict)

### 1. No Latin Transliteration
- **Rule:** Use Cyrillic ONLY.
- **Reason:** B1+ learners must read Cyrillic fluently. Transliteration (e.g., "dya-ku-yu") is a crutch that retards progress.
- **Exception:** Only A1.1 (M01-M05) allows limited transliteration for phonetics.

### 2. Anti-Surzhyk & Anti-Russification
- **Rule:** Zero tolerance for Surzhyk or Russian loanwords.
- **Calque Detection:** If a phrase is a direct loan translation from English (calque), identify it and teach the natural Ukrainian equivalent.
  - *Calque (Bad):* "–Ø –±–µ—Ä—É –∞–≤—Ç–æ–±—É—Å" (I take a bus).
  - *Natural (Good):* "–Ø —ó–¥—É –∞–≤—Ç–æ–±—É—Å–æ–º" (I go by bus).

### 3. Stylistic Syntax (Active Voice)
- **Rule:** Prioritize **active verbal constructions** over passive or nominal constructions.
- **Reason:** Ukrainian favors direct action. Passive voice with instrumental agent ("–Ω–∞–º–∏ –±—É–ª–æ –≤–∏—Ä—ñ—à–µ–Ω–æ") is heavy, bureaucratic, and influenced by Russian officialese.
  - *Bureaucratic (Avoid):* "–ù–∞–º–∏ –±—É–ª–æ –≤–∏—Ä—ñ—à–µ–Ω–æ –∑–º—ñ–Ω–∏—Ç–∏ –ø–ª–∞–Ω."
  - *Natural (Use):* "–ú–∏ –≤–∏—Ä—ñ—à–∏–ª–∏ –∑–º—ñ–Ω–∏—Ç–∏ –ø–ª–∞–Ω."

**Honesty First:** If you are unsure of a word, stress, or nuance, **admit it**. Never invent information.
